Sheet 8: Recursive Ray Tracing

Exercise: Recursive ray tracing (20 points)

The ray tracer classes from the previous sheet have been updated and now serve as the basis for this exercise. This sheet extends the system with recursive ray tracing.

So far, you have only traced primary rays from the camera to the objects. Now you will add secondary, tertiary, and recursively nested rays for reflection and refraction.

The starter code already traces primary rays from the camera, computes intersections with the scene, and evaluates local Phong lighting at the hit point. Your task is to extend this code so additional rays can be generated at a surface point: reflected rays, and, when the material supports it, refracted rays. The returned color is no longer only the local Phong color; it is a combination of local lighting and recursively computed contributions.

This implementation intentionally uses several approximations. They keep the code short, make the control flow easier to understand, and avoid some of the complexity of a physically accurate renderer. The tradeoff is that some choices are not fully physically correct. The goal of this exercise is not a perfect light-transport simulation, but a clear and manageable implementation of recursive ray tracing.

Ray-traced images of the test scene for recursion depths 2 through 9.

Implement recursive reflection and refraction for all materials that require them. Recursion must stop at a predefined depth so ray tracing always terminates after a finite number of secondary interactions. At the current hit point X, the final color CX is a combination of local lighting CX,local, reflected radiance CX,reflected, and transmitted radiance CX,transmitted. The factors l, r, and t denote the weights of local lighting, reflection, and transmission: 

Cₓ = l · Cₓ,local + r · Cₓ,reflected + t · Cₓ,transmitted

As a first approximation, we introduce an artificial distinction between three classes of objects in the scene. This simplifies the implementation and makes the system more efficient, but it is not fully physically correct. In reality, reflection and refraction occur together, so this separation is a modeling compromise rather than a physical truth. For this exercise it is useful because it keeps the recursion logic simple and easy to inspect. When making decisions in the code, follow this simplified model instead of trying to interpret every case physically.

We distinguish the following three material types:

  • local lighting only (red sphere)
  • local lighting + reflection + refraction (blue sphere)
  • local lighting + reflection (yellow sphere and floor)

To represent this simplified behavior, each material has an artificial parameter local. It specifies how much of the final color comes from local lighting, meaning the Phong lighting model already implemented in the starter code. The remaining fraction 1 - local is available for recursive effects. Depending on the material class, that remainder is assigned entirely to reflection or split between reflection and refraction. Intuitively, local describes how much of the surface appearance stays local, while 1 - local describes how much is contributed by secondary rays.

Materials with local lighting only do not spawn secondary rays. Therefore l = 1, r = 0, and t = 0.

For reflective and refractive materials, the non-local part is split between reflection and refraction, so 1 - l = r + t. The ratio between r and t is computed with the Fresnel equations, here using Schlick's approximation. R0 is the reflectivity at normal incidence, theta is the angle between the incoming ray direction and the surface normal, and n is the relative index of refraction. Under the simplifying assumption that one side of the interface is always air, compute n from the material IOR. Use the correct ratio depending on whether the ray enters or exits the object: 

R(θ) = R₀ + (1 - R₀)·(1 - cos(θ))⁵
R₀ = ((n - 1)/(n + 1))²
T(θ) = 1 - R(θ)
        
r = (1 - l) · R(θ)
t = (1 - l) · T(θ)

For reflective but non-refractive materials, such as the yellow sphere, use R0 = 1 - local, r = R(theta), l = 1 - r, and t = 0. This is also a deliberate simplification. A physically based derivation of a purely reflective material would look different, but for this exercise it is enough to treat the non-local part entirely as reflection.

You may assume that:

  • one side of each material interface is always air
  • objects do not overlap or intersect
  • models are closed (watertight) and all normals point outward

Use Vec3 to compute refracted rays. Note that theta is the angle between the incoming direction vector i and the surface normal n. Therefore cos(theta) is given by their dot product: cos(theta) = i * n.

Implementation hint: the starter code already computes the local Phong lighting term in Scene::traceRay. Keep that part and extend it with recursive reflection, recursive refraction, and the final combination of local and recursive contributions.

In concrete terms, the missing parts are:

  • add a recursion termination condition at the beginning of Scene::traceRay
  • create and trace a reflected secondary ray for reflective materials
  • create and trace a refracted secondary ray for refractive materials
  • choose the weights l, r, and t depending on the material type
  • combine localColor, reflected color, and refracted color into the final result
  • extend Material with a helper function that computes reflectivity using Schlick's approximation

One practical hint: under the assumptions of this exercise, the sign of the dot product between ray direction and surface normal tells you whether a ray is entering or leaving an object. When creating a secondary ray, also offset its origin by a small epsilon so numerical imprecision does not make it immediately hit the same surface again.
